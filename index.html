🚨 Breaches & Active Threats
Notable

New "Spirals" ransomware encrypts a victim network in under 24 hours

📅 Jul 16, 2026

Symantec's Threat Hunter Team traced a Spirals intrusion at a South Asian IT services firm from an exposed IIS server to full encryption in under a day: a web shell, UAC bypass, RDP enablement, a new local account, lateral movement to a dozen-plus hosts via WMI, and redundant remote-access channels (revsocks, Chisel, Cloudflare tunnels) before the Rust-based payload dropped.

Why it matters: Sub-24-hour dwell time leaves almost no window for manual detection and response — this is another data point that ransomware defense now has to assume automated, near-real-time containment rather than next-business-day triage.

US charges Russian operators of Medialand/ML.Cloud "bulletproof hosting" services

📅 Jul 15, 2026

Federal prosecutors unsealed an indictment against Alexander Volosovik, Kirill Zatolokin and Yulia Pankova, plus their St. Petersburg-based firms Medialand LLC and ML.Cloud LLC, accusing them of leasing take-down-resistant infrastructure across Russia, China, Finland and the Netherlands that powered ransomware, phishing and malware campaigns netting over $62 million from victims. A $10 million reward is now on offer for further information.

Why it matters: Bulletproof hosting is the plumbing behind a huge share of ransomware and phishing infrastructure — disrupting the providers, not just individual campaigns, is one of the few actions that raises costs across the entire criminal ecosystem at once.

Sophos: identity attacks overtake exploits as the top ransomware root cause

📅 Jul 15, 2026

Sophos's latest incident-response data shows 79% of ransomware attacks now originate from compromised identities — malicious email (26%), phishing (24%) and stolen credentials (23%) combined — while vulnerability exploitation fell to 18% from 32% previously. MFA was deployed in 97% of the credential-based incidents studied but still failed to stop the compromise.

Why it matters: "MFA deployed" clearly isn't the same as "MFA enforced everywhere" — the data argues for shifting ransomware-prevention budget from patch cadence toward identity threat detection, phishing-resistant MFA coverage gaps, and auditing non-human credentials.

🤖 AI Security
Critical

xAI's Grok Build CLI uploaded entire Git repositories — secrets included — despite privacy toggle

📅 Jul 14, 2026

Researcher CEREBLAB's wire-level (mitmproxy) analysis of Grok Build CLI v0.2.93 found it packaging developers' full tracked repositories, including committed secrets and Git history, and sending them to a Google Cloud Storage bucket — roughly 27,800x the data the coding task needed. Disabling xAI's "Improve the model" training-consent toggle had no effect on the uploads. xAI silently disabled the behavior on July 13 after the research reached Hacker News's front page and Musk promised to purge previously uploaded data.

Why it matters: A training-consent toggle and a data-transmission control are not the same setting — verify what your AI coding tools actually send over the wire rather than trusting a privacy switch's label, especially for tools with access to your source and secrets.
Source
Notable

"GhostApproval" trust-boundary flaw hits six major AI coding assistants

📅 Jul 8, 2026

Wiz Research disclosed a symlink-based technique letting a malicious repository trick Amazon Q Developer, Claude Code, Cursor, Google Antigravity, Windsurf and Augment into writing an attacker-supplied key to a sensitive location — while the approval dialog shown to the developer displayed only the harmless-looking target filename, bypassing informed consent. Amazon, Google and Cursor shipped fixes; Augment remains in progress and Windsurf has gone quiet since acknowledging the report in June.

Why it matters: If an AI coding agent's own permission-approval UI can't show you what it's actually about to touch, "the developer approved it" isn't a meaningful safeguard — treat agent file-write approvals as untrusted until vendors close this class of gap.
