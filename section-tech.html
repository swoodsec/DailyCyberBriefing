Major

Microsoft cuts 4,800 jobs, guts Xbox, and spins off four game studios in its biggest gaming overhaul ever

📅 Jul 6, 2026

Microsoft eliminated about 4,800 roles (2.1% of its global workforce), hitting Xbox and commercial sales hardest — 1,600 Xbox jobs cut immediately, rising to roughly 3,200 (20% of the division) through fiscal 2027. Compulsion Games and Double Fine return to independent ownership, while Ninja Theory and Undead Labs move to new owners; Xbox CEO Asha Sharma called margins "3-10x lower than comparable platform businesses" amid a worsening console hardware crisis.

Why it matters: It's the clearest sign yet that gaming hardware economics have buckled under AI-driven memory costs, and a major test of whether Microsoft can keep Xbox's content (Minecraft, Candy Crush, Game Pass) profitable while shedding studios and platform ambitions.

BAE- and NATO-backed VC fund raises €197M for European defense startups

📅 Jul 7, 2026

Expeditions, a venture firm backed by BAE Systems and the NATO Innovation Fund, closed a €197 million ($225M) fund aimed at up to 40 early-stage European startups building technology with "immediate operational relevance" to the continent's defense gaps. It lands amid a record year for defense-tech venture funding globally, with more than $14.6B committed in the first five months of 2026 alone.

Why it matters: Defense tech has become one of the hottest venture categories of 2026, and a dedicated European fund signals the continent wants sovereign alternatives to US "neo-primes" like Anduril rather than relying on them.

Apple lobbies Washington to buy memory chips from Pentagon-blacklisted Chinese makers

📅 Jul 2, 2026

Tim Cook has personally appealed to Trump administration officials, including Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent, seeking assurances before Apple signs supply deals with CXMT and YMTC — two Chinese memory makers on a Pentagon blacklist — to ease an AI-driven memory shortage that has already pushed some Apple product prices up as much as 50%.

Why it matters: It's a rare case of a hardware giant openly courting sanctioned suppliers to survive a components crunch, and a live test of how far Washington will bend export-control politics for Apple's supply chain.

Valve's $1,049 Steam Machine sold out in minutes — engineers explain the pricing and delays

📅 Jul 5, 2026

Valve engineers Pierre-Loup Griffais and Yazan Aldehayyat detailed the Steam Machine's pricing, compact design, and Windows support in a Tom's Hardware interview, after the console — delayed twice by the same memory shortage squeezing the rest of the industry — launched June 22 at $1,049 for the 512GB model and sold out almost immediately. Aldehayyat said the hardware program has to be "self-sustained," not subsidized by software sales.

Why it matters: A niche PC-gaming box selling out despite a $1,000-plus price tag shows demand for SteamOS hardware is real — and underscores how badly component shortages are reshaping console economics industry-wide.

📅 Jun 30, 2026

Why it matters: A retro-phone maker resorting to recycled RAM to hit a launch price is a small but vivid data point on how far the AI-driven memory shortage has squeezed even niche consumer hardware.

Notable

China's Tianwen-2 and Japan's Hayabusa2 both reach near-Earth asteroids this month

📅 Jul 6, 2026

CNSA's Tianwen-2 mission arrived at the near-Earth asteroid Kamo'oalewa — a quasi-satellite of Earth — to begin sample-collection operations using onboard spectrometers, a magnetometer, and a dust analyzer, while JAXA's Hayabusa2 conducted a flyby of near-Earth asteroid Torifune, giving scientists their first close-up data on both objects.

Why it matters: Two independent Asian space agencies reaching separate near-Earth asteroids in the same window marks a maturing era of routine, competitive asteroid science — with implications for planetary-defense and resource research alike.

Notable

A Falcon 9 flies two microwave-sized semiconductor "Fabships" on its booster alongside 29 Starlinks

📅 Jul 4, 2026

SpaceX's Starlink 10-50 mission from Cape Canaveral carried 29 satellites plus two "Clipper Class" Fabship pods for D.C. startup Besxar Space Industries, which hitched an ~8-minute suborbital ride on the first stage to test how terrestrially made semiconductor wafers survive launch and reentry. Besxar has booked 12 Falcon 9 flights to develop space-based chip-substrate manufacturing.

Why it matters: Manufacturing in orbit's microgravity and vacuum is an emerging frontier, and piggybacking experiments on reusable boosters is a cheap path to prove out whether space-made chips are viable.
