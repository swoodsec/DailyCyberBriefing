🚨 Breaches & Active Threats
Notable

18-agency advisory: Russian FSB hackers exploiting vulnerable routers to infiltrate critical infrastructure

📅 Jul 13, 2026

The NSA, FBI and CISA joined 15 additional agencies from the UK, Canada, Australia, New Zealand and several European nations in a joint advisory attributing an active campaign against poorly configured and unpatched routers to Russia's FSB Center 16, aimed at establishing footholds inside critical-infrastructure networks. The advisory includes detection guidance and urges immediate router hardening — disabling unused management interfaces, patching firmware and rotating default credentials.

Why it matters: An 18-agency joint advisory is a rare, high-confidence signal that a specific nation-state unit is actively working its way into critical infrastructure right now, not a retrospective report on last year's campaign.

Critical

Progress orders ShareFile customers to shut down Storage Zone Controllers over "credible" threat — no patch yet

📅 Jul 10, 2026

Progress Software told customers running ShareFile's self-hosted Storage Zone Controllers to immediately power down the Windows servers hosting them after identifying a credible external security threat, temporarily disabling cloud access to affected accounts. No CVE, technical root cause or patch has been published — an instruction to shut down rather than patch signals no fix currently exists.

Why it matters: Storage Zone Controllers typically sit at the network edge handling internet-facing file-sharing traffic; ordering a full shutdown rather than issuing a patch is one of the most serious vendor advisories of the year, and it leaves affected organizations with no remediation path but downtime.

Device-code phishing kits surge 37x as the technique goes from espionage tool to criminal commodity

📅 Jul 12, 2026

Researchers now track 18 distinct device-code phishing kits — branded VENOM, SHAREFILE, CLURE, FLOW_TOKEN and others — up sharply from a handful of Russian state-linked campaigns at the start of 2026, with every major adversary-in-the-middle phishing platform now adding the technique. The method abuses Microsoft's device-code OAuth flow to bypass passwords, MFA and even passkeys entirely.

Why it matters: A technique that bypasses phishing-resistant MFA and passkeys is exactly the kind of authentication-layer attack that renders years of credential-hardening investment moot — and it just went mainstream.

Israeli payments giant Nayax fights extortion threat as hacker claims a year of access, 1 billion card records

📅 Jul 8, 2026

A threat actor calling itself "TheSyndicate" claims nearly a year of undetected access inside Nayax's systems and is threatening to leak over 100TB of data — including a claimed 1 billion card records, KYC data, source code and infrastructure maps — on July 21 unless paid. Nayax confirmed detecting and blocking anomalous activity in one subsidiary's cloud account but says core payment-processing systems were unaffected; the attacker's broader claims remain unverified.

Why it matters: Even unverified, a claimed year-long dwell time inside a global payments processor with a public leak deadline is worth tracking closely as July 21 approaches — treat the billion-record figure as an unconfirmed claim, not a fact, until data surfaces.

"GodDamn" ransomware uses a Microsoft-signed driver to blind EDR before encrypting

📅 Jul 9, 2026

The newest rebrand of the Monster/Beast ransomware lineage loads a malicious, Microsoft-signed kernel driver called PoisonX to strip EDR tools of the kernel callbacks they rely on for detection — leaving dashboards showing "healthy" while process, driver-load and file-activity alerts go dark. Attackers paired it with AnyDesk remote access and a Symantec-disguised evasion tool before deploying the ransomware.

Why it matters: A signed driver getting past Microsoft's own attestation process to blind EDR at the kernel level is a reminder that "signed" doesn't mean "safe" — bring-your-own-vulnerable-driver attacks remain one of ransomware's most effective defense-evasion tricks.

Debian 13.6 ships 120 security fixes and a critical patch for the expiring 2013 Secure Boot certificate

📅 Jul 13, 2026

The sixth point release of Debian 13 "trixie" bundles 120 security advisories and 124 bug fixes across widely used packages including the Linux kernel, OpenSSL, Samba, Apache, Nginx, Redis and PostgreSQL, alongside an fwupd update that can refresh the industry's expiring 2013 UEFI Secure Boot certificate authority before it breaks future Secure Boot bootloader updates.

Why it matters: The Secure Boot CA fix matters well beyond Debian — it addresses an industry-wide expiring-certificate problem that could otherwise leave Secure Boot-enabled systems unable to apply future bootloader updates.

Critical

"GhostLock": 15-year-old Linux kernel flaw grants root and container escape on virtually every distro

📅 Jul 7, 2026

Nebula Security's VEGA research team disclosed CVE-2026-43499, a use-after-free bug in the kernel's futex priority-inheritance path present since Linux 2.6.39 (2011), turning it into a 97%-reliable root exploit that also escapes containers and works on Android; Google paid $92,337 through its kernelCTF program. Major distros are still rolling out fixes weeks later.

Why it matters: A 15-year-old bug requiring no special permissions or network access, reliably exploitable in seconds and reachable from inside a container, is about as close to a worst-case local-privilege-escalation scenario as Linux gets — check patch status now rather than assuming someone else already did.

Critical

"Friendly Fire": AI coding agents tricked into running attacker code while scanning for vulnerabilities

📅 Jul 9, 2026

AI Now Institute researchers showed that asking Claude Code or OpenAI's Codex to review an open-source library for security issues can instead get the agent to execute a hidden payload — the proof-of-concept hid a malicious binary beside a legitimate one, disguised well enough that the agent's own disassembly check treated them as related, then ran it "as a routine check." The exploit transferred directly from Claude Code to Codex without modification.

Why it matters: This isn't a patchable bug in either product — it's the same underlying weakness researchers keep finding: frontier models can't reliably tell a trusted instruction from untrusted data smuggled in through the very code they're asked to review.

"WriteOut": one shared link could hijack any Writer AI organization via a cross-tenant session leak

📅 Jul 7, 2026

Sand Security disclosed a now-patched flaw in enterprise AI platform Writer where a victim who simply opened an attacker-shared agent preview link had their live session cookie forwarded into that sandboxed preview — handing the attacker full account takeover, including private chats, documents, connectors and LLM credentials, and potentially admin control. Writer fixed it by isolating preview sessions onto a separate origin.

Why it matters: A one-click, no-warning cross-tenant takeover in a widely deployed enterprise AI platform shows how "preview" and "share" features common across AI products can silently break tenant isolation if session handling isn't airtight.

📅 Jul 8, 2026
