Coca-Cola's Fairlife dairy unit halts U.S. production after ransomware attack

📅 Jul 16, 2026

Coca-Cola disclosed in an SEC 8-K filing that Fairlife detected unauthorized access to systems — including production systems — tied to a ransomware attack, temporarily suspending U.S. manufacturing while Canadian operations continue unaffected. The company says product quality and safety are not impacted, has notified law enforcement, and has not yet disclosed whether data was stolen or a ransom demanded.

Why it matters: A ransomware hit that reaches OT/production systems at a household-name manufacturer — not just IT — is a reminder that segmentation between corporate networks and the plant floor is what determines whether an intrusion becomes an inconvenience or a supply disruption.

Scattered Spider duo jailed 66 months over £29M Transport for London hack

📅 Jul 16, 2026

Thalha Jubair, 20, and Owen Flowers, 18, were each sentenced to five years and six months under Section 3ZA of the UK's Computer Misuse Act for the August 2024 TfL intrusion that hit 148 systems, forced a password reset for all 27,000 employees, and disrupted Dial-a-Ride, concessionary travel cards and refunds. Investigators estimated a full network outage could have cost the UK economy up to £56 billion.

Why it matters: This is only the second-ever conviction under the CMA's toughest provision and Britain's biggest cybercrime prosecution to date — a real deterrence data point as Scattered Spider's helpdesk-impersonation playbook keeps spreading.

Notable

Microsoft: compromised AsyncAPI npm packages deliver malware at import time, not install

📅 Jul 15, 2026

Microsoft Threat Intelligence traced a coordinated compromise of five package versions across the @asyncapi npm organization — pulled via a poisoned pull request — that trigger a hidden malicious loader on require()/import rather than a postinstall hook, meaning the common npm install --ignore-scripts mitigation doesn't stop it. The loader fetches a second-stage payload from IPFS and is detected by Defender as Trojan:JS/MiasmStealer.SC.

Why it matters: An import-time trigger defeats the standard "disable install scripts" supply-chain defense — teams need to also pin exact versions, purge caches, and hunt for the malware's OS-specific masquerade directories rather than relying on one mitigation.

🛡️ Vulnerabilities & Patches
Critical

SonicWall SMA1000 zero-days (CVSS 10.0) actively exploited; federal deadline is today

📅 Jul 14, 2026

CVE-2026-15409, a critical unauthenticated SSRF in the SMA1000 Work Place interface, and CVE-2026-15410, a post-auth code-injection flaw in the Management Console, are being actively exploited against SMA6210/7210/8200v appliances to steal credentials, session databases and TOTP MFA seeds. Both are now in CISA's KEV catalog, and Binding Operational Directive 26-04 requires federal agencies to remediate or discontinue use by July 17.

Why it matters: A CVSS-10 pre-auth bug on internet-facing remote-access gear is a worst-case combination — if you run SMA1000, patch to the hotfix releases today, check the published IOCs, and assume compromise (re-image and rotate credentials/TOTP seeds) rather than just patching.

CISA adds exploited SharePoint deserialization zero-day and two FortiSandbox flaws to KEV

📅 Jul 16, 2026

CVE-2026-58644 (CVSS 9.8), a SharePoint Server deserialization bug patched in July's Patch Tuesday, was confirmed exploited as a zero-day before the fix shipped and joins CVE-2026-32201/45659/56164 in enabling RCE and IIS machine-key theft on on-prem SharePoint. The same day, CISA also added two critical FortiSandbox command-injection bugs (CVE-2026-25089, CVE-2026-39808) following continued active exploitation reports.

Why it matters: Four separate SharePoint CVEs are now confirmed exploited in the same campaign — patching the newest one isn't enough; CISA's guidance to rotate IIS machine keys and verify AMSI integration applies across the whole chain, not just the latest CVE.

🤖 AI Security
Critical

Hugging Face discloses intrusion driven end-to-end by an autonomous AI agent

📅 Jul 16, 2026

Hugging Face says a malicious dataset abused a remote-code dataset loader and a template-injection bug to run code on a processing worker, then an autonomous agent framework — executing thousands of actions across a swarm of short-lived sandboxes with self-migrating C2 — escalated to node-level access and moved laterally over a weekend. Hugging Face itself used an open-weight model (GLM 5.2) run on its own infrastructure to analyze the 17,000-event attacker log, after hosted frontier-model APIs refused to process the raw attack commands as a safety-guardrail false positive.

Why it matters: This is a rare first-party admission that an "agentic attacker" scenario the industry has only forecast is now operational — and the finding that defenders' own hosted-AI guardrails can block legitimate forensic analysis of attack logs is a planning gap worth closing before your own incident, not during it.

Notable

Check Point's 2026 AI Security Report: "AI used to help plan the break-in, now it's doing the break-in"

📅 Jul 15, 2026

Check Point Research documented year-long intrusions where AI ran exploitation workflows autonomously — generating thousands of commands across dozens of sessions with minimal human direction — and found 87-93% of organizations had at least one high-risk AI data-exposure interaction each month between October 2025 and May 2026. A persistent jailbreak technique now plants malicious instructions in auto-loaded files like CLAUDE.md rather than relying on prompts that get patched.

Why it matters: The report's core claim — that the skill gap separating capable attackers from novices is disappearing — argues for auditing which auto-loaded config/instruction files your AI coding and analysis tools trust by default, since that's now a persistence mechanism, not just a convenience feature.

Update

Tracebit's "context bombs" cut AI-attacker success rates by up to 90% in cyber-range tests

📅 Jul 14, 2026

Tracebit researchers planted decoy credentials containing a "context bomb" — text designed to trigger an offensive AI agent's own safety guardrails — and ran 152 attack sessions across five frontier models (Opus 4.8, Gemini 3.1 Pro, GLM 5.2, DeepSeek 4 Pro, Kimi K2.6) against a simulated AWS environment. Opus 4.8 went from 93% full-admin success in clean runs to 0% once a context bomb was present; across all five models, attack-path success fell from 91% to 15%.

Why it matters: Flipping prompt injection from an attacker's tool into a defender's tripwire is a genuinely new deception technique — worth testing against your own canary/honeytoken strategy, though it's not a silver bullet against "abliterated" models stripped of safety guardrails, which the researchers haven't yet tested.
